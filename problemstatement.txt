PRODUCT MANAGER ASSIGNMENT
Design a Daily Energy Coach
An open-ended product exercise
Time expected 4 to 6 hours
Submission A working prototype, plus a short written walkthrough. The prototype
can be built in any tool you like (v0, Figma Make, Lovable, Bolt, Cursor,
plain HTML, Replit, or anything else). It does not need to be production
code. It does need to be clickable and demo-able.
Discussion 60 minute walkthrough with the product team
The Problem
Most people working ‘knowledge jobs’ today are running on borrowed energy. They wake
up tired, stack their hardest work into their worst hours, eat lunch at their desk, push
through an afternoon crash with caffeine, and end the day too wired to sleep well, only to
start the cycle again tomorrow.
They have access to more data about themselves than any generation in history. Their
phones know their step count, their watches know their heart rate, their calendars know
their meeting load, their laptops know when they were typing and when they were idle. But
none of this data tells them the one thing they actually want to know: what should I do
today, right now, to feel better and do better work?
Existing tools fail this person in predictable ways. Calendar apps schedule time but ignore
biology. Wearables show charts but never tell you what to do. Wellness apps ask you to log
everything manually, then quietly die when you stop. Productivity apps optimise tasks
without knowing whether you have any energy left to do them.
There is a real gap here. Someone needs to build a product that closes it.
Your Assignment
Design that product, then build a working prototype of it.
You have full creative freedom. You decide what it is called, what it does, what it looks like,
who it is for, and how it makes money. You decide what data it uses and how it gets that
data. You decide what the user opens on day one, what keeps them coming back on day
thirty, and what makes them tell a friend on day ninety.
The end output we want to see is a clickable prototype we can actually open and move
through, paired with a short written walkthrough explaining the choices behind it. The
prototype can be built in any tool you are fluent in. Use AI builders, no-code tools, design
tools, or write the code yourself. We do not care how it was made. We care that we can
experience it.
We are not looking for the right answer. There are many possible right answers. We are
looking for the quality of your thinking, the choices you make, and the things you choose to
leave out.
What to Submit
Two things: the prototype itself, and a short written walkthrough (no more than two pages)
covering the five areas below. How you structure the walkthrough is up to you.
1. The user and the moment
Who is this product for? Be specific. Pick one real person you can describe in concrete
detail, including what their Tuesday morning actually looks like and where in that morning
your product enters their life. Tell us why they would care enough to install it, and what
they are using today instead.
2. The product itself
What does the product actually do? Your prototype should show the core experience: what
a user sees when they open it, the one moment that defines the product, and the loop that
brings them back. The written walkthrough should call out the single interaction that
matters most and explain why.
Be explicit about three things: what data the product uses, how it gets that data, and what
the user has to do versus what happens automatically. The line between active input and
passive sensing is the most important design decision in this space. Tell us where you draw
it and why.
3. The hero metric
Pick one number that this product centres itself around. One number the user sees every
day and starts to care about over time. Something that summarises whether today is a
good day or a bad day in this product's worldview.
Tell us what the number is, how it is calculated at a conceptual level, why this number and
not a different one, and what could go wrong if a user starts optimising for it in unhealthy
ways.
4. The retention problem
Most products in this category lose more than 80 percent of users inside 90 days. The
pattern is consistent across wellness apps, productivity tools, and habit trackers. People
install them on a Sunday full of intent and stop opening them by the second Wednesday.
Tell us why your product will not die that way. What is the specific reason a real human will
still be opening it on day thirty when their initial motivation has worn off? Do not give us a
list of features. Give us the one mechanism, behavioural or structural, that breaks the
pattern.
5. What you would build first
If you were given one engineer and twelve weeks to ship a v1 of this product, what would
you cut and what would you keep? What is the smallest version of this product that still
proves the core idea is real? What would you measure in week thirteen to know whether to
keep going or kill it?
How We Will Evaluate Your Response
Imagination Did you build something interesting, or did you describe a slightly
better version of something that already exists? The brief is
deliberately open. We want to see what you do with that space.
Product instinct Do your decisions feel like they come from someone who has
actually watched users abandon products and understands why?
Can you predict how a real human will behave with what you have
designed?
Depth When we push on any one of your choices in the discussion, will
there be a real reason underneath? Or will we hit air? We are looking
for ideas you have actually thought through to the second and third
level.
Communication Can you explain a complicated product simply? Could a senior leader
skim your submission in five minutes and walk away knowing exactly
what you would build and why?
Honesty Are you willing to name the parts of your idea that are weak, the
assumptions you are making, and the things you would not build?
We hire people who can disagree with themselves.
A Few Notes
• Use any tools you want, including AI builders, design tools, and code assistants. We
assume you will. We are interested in how you use them, not whether.
• The prototype does not need to be polished or fully functional. It needs to
communicate the idea clearly enough that we can experience it. One well-executed
flow beats five half-built screens.
• Do not let the prototype eat all your time. The thinking matters more than the pixels.
If your written walkthrough is weak, no amount of UI polish will save it.
• If you think we are asking the wrong question, answer the question you think we
should have asked instead. Be explicit about the swap.
• If something is unclear, email us before you start. Asking nothing is not a virtue here.
We look forward to reading what you make.
